Drywall damage happens in every home. Kids, doorknobs, furniture moves, water leaks, or just years of wear — eventually something puts a hole or crack in a wall, and then you have to figure out what to do about it.

The good news is that most drywall damage is fixable without tearing anything out. The tricky part is knowing what kind of fix actually makes sense for the damage you have. A small nail hole needs something completely different than a water-damaged section of ceiling — and doing the wrong thing can cost you more time and money down the road.

This post breaks down the most common types of drywall damage and what the right repair looks like for each one.

---

## Small Holes and Dents

These are the most common repairs — nail holes from picture hangings, small punctures, dings from furniture bumping into walls, or screw heads that popped through the surface.

For holes up to about an inch across, the fix is simple: a bit of joint compound, let it dry, sand it smooth, and touch up the paint. Most homeowners can do this themselves with a tube of pre-mixed compound and some patience.

For holes up to about four to six inches, the process is similar but usually involves a mesh patch or a California patch (where you cut a piece of drywall with a paper flap that gets embedded into the compound). This takes a little more skill to get a flat, invisible result, but it's still a routine repair.

The main thing that complicates small patches isn't the repair itself — it's the texture and paint matching afterward. If the wall has no texture and you have the right paint, a small patch is nearly invisible. If the wall has a knock-down or orange peel texture, matching it takes more care.

## Medium Drywall Damage

Anything larger than about six inches starts to fall into a different category. This includes damage from a doorknob that's been hitting the wall for years, sections that got damaged during a move, or areas where someone ran conduit or cable through the wall and left a rough opening.

For repairs in this range, we usually cut the damaged section back to the nearest studs and install a new piece of drywall. This gives the patch something solid to attach to and makes the repair more durable than trying to bridge a large gap with compound alone.

The result, done right, should be invisible after texture and paint. But "medium" repairs tend to require two coats of compound with drying time between them, which can mean two visits if the schedule doesn't allow for a single long day on site.

## Water-Damaged Drywall

Water damage is in a different category from regular drywall repair, and it's worth treating it that way.

If drywall has gotten wet — from a roof leak, a plumbing failure, or a slow drip that went unnoticed — the question isn't just about fixing the surface. You need to know that the source of moisture has been dealt with before closing anything up. Patching over active moisture is a short-term fix that usually leads to mold, and mold is a much bigger problem than a stained wall.

Once the source is confirmed to be resolved and the area has dried out, water-damaged drywall is assessed based on what's left:

- **Staining only, no structural softness** — The drywall may still be sound. In some cases, a stain-blocking primer and fresh paint is enough. In others, the stained section gets cut out and replaced.
- **Soft or crumbling drywall** — The material itself has deteriorated and needs to come out. There's no patching soft drywall; it has to be replaced.
- **Mold present** — This needs to be handled properly before any drywall work happens. The affected material gets removed, the framing gets treated, and new drywall goes in after everything is clean and dry.

If you see brown or yellow water staining on walls or ceilings, take it seriously and find out where it came from before calling for a drywall patch.

## Cracks in Walls or Ceilings

Cracks are common, but they're not all the same. Some are cosmetic. Some are a sign of movement in the structure.

**Hairline cracks** along paint seams or at ceiling-wall joints are usually just settling. Homes move a little over time, and these cracks show up, especially in older construction. A thin bead of paintable caulk or a skim of joint compound, followed by paint, typically takes care of them.

**Wider cracks or cracks with displacement** — where one side of the crack is higher than the other — warrant a closer look. These can indicate foundation movement, framing issues, or significant settling. We can patch the drywall, but if the underlying cause isn't addressed, the crack comes back. If you're seeing wide cracks with displacement, it's worth having a foundation or structural professional take a look before you invest in cosmetic repairs.

**Cracks at corners** — the inside corner where two walls meet, or where the wall meets the ceiling — are usually just tape failure. The tape embedded in the joint compound has lifted or separated, and it needs to be re-taped and re-mudded.

## Texture Matching

Texture matching is one of the most underestimated parts of drywall repair. Getting the patch to disappear requires more than just covering the hole — the texture has to match what's on the surrounding wall.

Common residential textures include orange peel, knockdown, skip trowel, and smooth (which is its own challenge). Each one is applied differently and blended differently.

An experienced tradesperson can usually get a very close match, but "exact match" is hard to guarantee on older walls. Paint that's faded over time, texture that's built up over multiple coats, and original textures applied by hand all make perfect matching difficult. In most cases the repair is invisible from a normal viewing distance. Up close under raking light, there may be a slight variation.

If you're particular about this — or if the wall is in a high-visibility area — it's worth discussing ahead of time so expectations are clear.

## When Repainting Is Needed

Not every drywall repair requires repainting the entire wall, but some do.

For small patches in an out-of-the-way spot with the right paint on hand, a touch-up often blends fine. For larger repairs, or repairs in the middle of a prominent wall, a touch-up rarely looks right. The repaired section gets primed and painted, and the sheen difference between fresh paint and aged paint becomes visible.

In those cases, painting the full wall from corner to corner gives a clean, consistent result. It costs more than a spot touch-up, but it avoids the patchy appearance that comes from painting over a specific section of a wall that's been there for years.

## When Drywall Should Be Replaced Instead of Patched

Patching is almost always cheaper than replacing, but there are situations where replacement is the smarter call:

- **Large areas of water damage** — Cutting out and replacing is cleaner and more reliable than trying to stabilize compromised material.
- **Mold contamination** — Affected drywall needs to come out completely.
- **Multiple repairs clustered in a small area** — A wall that's been patched repeatedly in the same section often looks better after a full panel replacement than after another round of patchwork.
- **Fire or smoke damage** — Beyond cosmetic repair and needs to be evaluated on a case-by-case basis.
- **Extremely old drywall with asbestos concerns** — Pre-1980 construction with certain materials may need testing before any disturbing work happens. Worth mentioning if you have a very old home.

In most cases, a good patch done right looks just as good as new drywall. Replacement is the right answer when the damage is too widespread or the material is no longer structurally sound.
